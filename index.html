Introduction

Traveller uses a unique method to create characters ready for play. Instead of choosing a class and various skills or abilities, in Traveller you start off as a bright-eyed 18 year old with the whole universe at your feet. You will then decide what kind of career you might want to engage in and then watch as your Traveller’s life unfolds – very possibly in a completely different direction!

When you have finished this process, you will be ready for play with a Traveller who has history and a full career (successful or otherwise) behind them. They will now be ready for the next phase of their life, one full of excitement and adventure…

It should be noted that there are no ‘super’ characters in Traveller. This game is very much based around the idea of putting ordinary people in extraordinary situations, and of playing with the cards that the universe has dealt to you. Be ready to make the best of what you get – much like real life (though real life tends to lack starships, exploding suns and eight foot tall intelligent lion-like aliens).

So, another player may end up with a battle-hardened marine who can shoot out a fox’s eye at a hundred paces with any weapon in Charted Space, but your failed rock star who never managed to get anywhere in his career may well have more to contribute to the group. After all, not every problem can be solved with a gun. In fact, in Traveller, a gun may cause more issues than it solves.

Characteristics

Every person and creature in Traveller has several characteristics that describe their base mental and physical potential. These are determined by rolling two D6, six different times, to generate six scores. You can then choose which of these values to apply to each statistic. These characteristics may change later during character creation.

A value of 7 is considered average, with 2 being the lowest possible value and 12 being the highest. As we roll two dice, extreme values are less likely. There's a 1 in 36 chance of getting 2 (You must roll two 1s), and a 1 in 6 chance of getting 7 (You can roll either a 1 and a 6, or a 2 and a 5, etc.)

Depending on the characteristic score, there is an associated dice modifier (DM). A score of 2 gives a DM of -2; a score of 3 to 5 gives a DM of -1; a score of 6 to 8 gives a DM of 0; a score of 9 to 11 gives a DM of +1; and a score of 12 gives a DM of +2.

These characteristics also affect your chance of qualifying and being promoted in various jobs in later sections, for instance to become a Merchant you roll two D6, and add your INT DM. If you score 4 or above you succed in becoming a Merchant. Hover over the characteristics below to see which jobs rely on them and the dice roll needed.

Starting Skills

Skills are the most important aspect of a Traveller, and represents how well they can perform various tasks.

If a character is untrained in a skill they suffer a DM-3 when trying to use that skill. If a character has level zero that means that they are competent in using that skill, but has had little experience. They do not get any bonuses but avoid the -3 penalty. If a traveller has one or more levels in a skill (Skill 1, Skill 2, etc.) they are trained. Levels 2-3 indicate a skilled profesional, and 4-5 mean the character is probably well respected and known at least planet wide. Each level indicates several years of experience using that skill, and grants DM+1 per level for any checks using that skill.

Some skills have specialities – specialised, more focussed forms of that skill. A Traveller picks a speciality when he gains level 1 in a skill that has two or more specialities. For example, a Traveller might have Engineer 0, allowing him to make Engineer skill checks without the unskilled penalty. He might then gain a level in Engineer, at which point he would need to choose a speciality. For Engineer, this is a choice between jump drive, manoeuvre drive, power plant and electronics.

Starting out

Next, your Traveller engages in one or more career terms, representing their previous experiences and adventures before the start of the campaign. Each career term lasts for four years, even if a Traveller fails a Survival roll or is forced to leave a career. At many points during a career, a Traveller will have to make a dice roll of some sort. Most of these rolls are based on characteristics – simply roll 2D, add the DM from the listed characteristic, and try to get a total higher than or equal to the listed value. So, a roll of INT 8+ means ‘roll 2D, add your Intellect DM, and you succeed if you roll an 8 or more’.

In each career, there are three assignments, representing different aspects of the career. Pick one of these assignments when you enter the career. For example under the Rogue career there are the assignments Thief, Enforcer or Pirate.

You can also choose to enter either University or Military Academy instead during your first, second or third term. If you fail entry during your first term you can try again in your second or third, however the dice roll gets harder in each subsequent year. If you fail to qualify you then attempt to join a career as normal. Attending university or Military Academy provides the chance to gain increases to your Education characteristic, as well as skills, bonuses to rolls to qualify for other careers, and the chance to roll for commission before the first term of a military career, entering as an officer.

Also, keep track of your age. After you turn 34 there is a small but increasing chance each term that you will lose points in one or more characteristics.

Career checklist

  • First, if it's in the first three terms, decide whether or not to apply University or Military Academy, and roll to see if you succeed in qualifying. If unsuccesful apply for a career as normal, otherwise choose which skills you learn, roll for events, and then roll for graduation. After your University/Military Academy proceed to the next term.
  • If not entering pre-career education, pick which career you would like to apply for. If this is your first time in this career, roll to qualify. If you fail to qualify either choose the drifter career or enter the draft to be randomly assigned a military career (You may only enter the draft once in your lifetime).
  • Each career has four or five types of skill table associated with it – Personal Development, Service Skills, Specialist Skills, officer skills and Advanced Education. In each term you spend in a career, pick one of these tables and roll 1D to see which skill you increase. If this is your first career you gain basic training in all of the service skills at level 0. In the first term of subsequent careers you pick only one service skill.
  • Next roll the survival check to see if you suffer a mishap and are forced to leave your career. Assuming you don't, roll the event check to see what interesting thing befalls you.
  • If you are in a military career, you can then roll for commission to become a rank 1 officer, allowing access to the officer tables. If your social standing is below 9 you may only roll for commission in your first term.
  • Every career has a mandatory roll for advancement, which is different to the commission. If you make a successful advancement roll you gain a rank in the career and get an extra roll on any of the skills and training tables as well as any bonuses listed. However, if your advancement roll is equal to or less than the number of terms you have spent in this career, then you cannot continue in this career after this term. Either your services are no longer required, or events have caused you to leave, or perhaps you are simply bored and want a new challenge. If you roll a natural 12, then you must continue in this career. You are too valuable to lose and will be strong-armed into staying.
  • Finally, decide whether to continue to the next term or finish character creation. When you leave a career for any reason, you gain material benefits from that career, representing money, equipment and influence acquired during your years of work. There are two tables for this in each career – Cash and Benefits. You may only roll on Cash tables a maximum of three times in your lifetime, regardless of how many careers or Benefit rolls you accumulate. You receive one Benefit roll per full term served. Leaving due to a mishap means you lose the Benefit roll for that term, but you still gain the benefit rolls for previous terms in that career. If you reached rank 1 or 2, you get an extra Benefit roll when leaving that career. If you reached rank 3 or 4, you get two extra Benefit rolls, and if you reached rank 5 or 6, you get three extra Benefit rolls and may also apply DM+1 to all rolls on the Cash and Benefits tables.
